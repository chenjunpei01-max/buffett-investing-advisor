# Buffett Principle Index

Use this file to translate Buffett/Berkshire ideas into practical judgment rules. Prefer paraphrase and cite official Berkshire source pages when needed:

- Shareholder letters: https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/letters.html
- Owner's Manual: https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/owners.html
- Annual reports: https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/reports.html

## Business Owner Mindset

- Ask whether the user would want to own the whole business for five to ten years.
- Treat price movement as an offer, not proof of value.
- Prefer stable business economics over narratives, themes, or market popularity.
- If a company cannot be understood well enough to estimate owner earnings, mark it outside or near the edge of `circle of competence`.

## Moat

Classify moat source:

- Brand and habit: consumer trust, status, taste, repeat purchase, distribution control.
- Cost advantage: structurally lower cost, scale purchasing, logistics, production efficiency.
- Network/platform effect: more users or suppliers make the product more valuable.
- Switching cost: customer workflow, data, regulation, integration, or habit makes replacement painful.
- Regulation/licensing: permission-based scarcity; discount this if political risk is high.

Moat warnings:

- A famous brand without pricing power is not a strong moat.
- Market share bought with subsidies or debt is not a durable moat.
- High margins in a fast-changing industry need extra proof of durability.

## Owner Earnings

Use accounting profit only as a starting point. Adjust mentally for:

- Maintenance capital expenditure.
- Working capital needs.
- Stock-based compensation and dilution.
- Cyclical peak margins.
- One-off gains/losses.
- Debt-funded growth or acquisitions.

For asset-light consumer franchises, owner earnings may approximate free cash flow over a cycle. For banks, insurers, real estate, utilities, and commodity businesses, adapt the concept carefully and avoid false precision.

## Management And Capital Allocation

Look for evidence in actions:

- Reinvestment where returns on capital remain high.
- Dividends when reinvestment opportunities are limited.
- Buybacks only when shares are below intrinsic value.
- Debt reduction when leverage threatens resilience.
- Acquisitions only when price and strategic fit are rational.

Red flags:

- Growth pursued for size rather than per-share value.
- Persistent dilution without clear per-share benefit.
- Promotional communication, adjusted metrics that hide economics, or refusal to discuss mistakes.
- Related-party deals that favor insiders over minority shareholders.

## Leverage And Fragility

Buffett-style analysis heavily penalizes avoidable ruin risk. Reduce confidence when:

- Debt maturities are close and refinancing depends on favorable markets.
- Cash flow is cyclical but debt is fixed.
- Inventory, receivables, guarantees, or off-balance-sheet obligations are large.
- The business needs continual external capital.

## Temperament

Use patience as an active decision:

- `观察` is a valid conclusion when quality is high but price is not attractive.
- Avoid upgrading a conclusion just because the stock has fallen.
- Avoid downgrading a high-quality business purely because of short-term price weakness, unless thesis evidence deteriorates.
- Require a larger margin of safety when emotions or market consensus are doing too much of the work.

## Opportunity Cost

Compare every idea against:

- Cash or short-duration risk-free yield.
- Broad index exposure.
- Existing best holding in the user's portfolio if provided.
- Another company with clearer economics and similar valuation.

An idea can be good and still not be the best use of capital.
