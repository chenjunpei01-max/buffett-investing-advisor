## Condensed Principles

- Think like a business owner, not a ticker trader. Evaluate whether the whole business is worth owning for years.
- Stay inside the `circle of competence`. A pass is better than a confident-looking guess outside what can be understood.
- Prefer businesses with durable competitive advantages, high returns on capital, pricing power, and low need for incremental capital.
- Demand honest and capable management, with rational capital allocation and shareholder-oriented communication.
- Treat accounting earnings as a starting point, not the destination. Estimate `owner earnings` after maintenance capital needs and working-capital demands.
- Use conservative assumptions. A purchase only becomes attractive when price is meaningfully below conservative value.
- Keep a `margin of safety`. The harder the business is to forecast, the larger the required discount.
- Avoid businesses where leverage, accounting opacity, commodity dependence, regulation, or technology disruption can permanently impair value.
- Consider opportunity cost. A merely fair idea may be unattractive if cash, an index, or an existing high-quality holding is better.
- Be patient. Inactivity is acceptable when price and quality do not align.

## Interpretation Rules

- "Wonderful company at a fair price" does not mean any price is acceptable. Tie quality to valuation.
- High growth is not enough. Check whether growth creates per-share value after dilution, reinvestment, debt, and cyclicality.
- Low price is not enough. A poor business can be cheap for good reason.
- Dividends and buybacks are only attractive when backed by sustainable owner earnings and rational reinvestment tradeoffs.
- Management quality must be evidenced by capital allocation, balance-sheet discipline, disclosure, and treatment of minority shareholders.
