# Market Adaptation Notes

## A-share

Use extra skepticism for accounting quality, shareholder behavior, and regulatory status:

- Check ST, delisting risk, exchange inquiries, regulatory penalties, restatements, auditor changes, and qualified opinions.
- Review receivables, inventory, capitalized costs, related-party transactions, guarantees, pledged shares, and large non-operating items.
- Treat high reported profit with weak operating cash flow as a major warning sign.
- Adjust for policy cycles, industry restrictions, state ownership, subsidies, and local government influence.
- For consumer or monopoly-like companies, still require valuation discipline; brand quality does not erase price risk.

## Hong Kong

Focus on liquidity, governance, and cross-border exposure:

- Check trading liquidity, bid-ask spread, free float, and whether the stock is investable at the user's likely size.
- Review controlling-shareholder behavior, related-party deals, privatization risk, and dividend history.
- Separate mainland business economics from Hong Kong listing mechanics.
- Consider currency, dividend withholding, and different investor-base valuation norms.
- Be careful with very low P/E or high dividend yield if governance, cyclicality, or capital structure is weak.

## U.S.

Use filings and per-share economics:

- Prioritize 10-K, 10-Q, proxy statements, segment notes, and management discussion.
- Check stock-based compensation, buybacks versus dilution, debt maturity, pension/lease obligations, and tax effects.
- For technology and platform companies, test moat durability, reinvestment returns, and disruption risk rather than assuming growth persists.
- For financials and insurers, adapt owner-earnings logic carefully; book value quality, underwriting, reserves, deposits, and leverage matter.
- Compare valuation to interest rates and opportunity cost; long-duration growth is more sensitive to discount rates.

## Cross-market Defaults

- Use local currency for company analysis, then translate only when needed for the user's portfolio view.
- State whether the conclusion is based on current verified data or stale/user-provided data.
- Avoid direct comparison of multiples across markets without adjusting for governance, liquidity, rates, tax, growth, and accounting differences.
- When information quality is poor, reduce confidence and demand a larger margin of safety.
